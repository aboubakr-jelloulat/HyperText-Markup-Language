GNU/Linux — A Short History

In 1983 Richard Stallman launched the GNU project to create a free Unix-like operating system; GNU stands for "GNU's Not Unix."

Stallman and the Free Software movement promoted software freedom: the rights to use, study, modify and share code.

GNU developed many essential tools (compilers, shells, libraries) but lacked a kernel for years.

In 1991 Linus Torvalds released the Linux kernel; when combined with GNU tools it formed a complete OS—often called "GNU/Linux."

The mentality behind GNU/Linux is collaboration, transparency, and preserving users' freedoms via licenses like the GPL (copyleft).

Today many communities and distributions build on these ideas, balancing practical needs with ethical principles.

GNU/Linux powers servers, desktops, phones and embedded systems—showing how shared work scales globally.

Its story is still active: people worldwide contribute, debate, and improve both code and the culture of free software.
